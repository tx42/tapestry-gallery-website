Tapestry

(c. 1500, Flanders)

The possible fragment of bigger tapestry is of style called millefleurs. Popular in 15th and 16th centuries, they depicted small animals and birds among the flowers.

Susanna and the Elders

(c. 1500, Tournai)

The biblical story of slander against Susana appears in Book of Daniel. There Elders, failing to press heroine into submission, arrested her with the claim that she was committing adultery.However, Daniel managed to uncover their scheme. Tapestries with this subject were popular from the late Middle Ages onwards.

La Main Chaude

(1500-1515, Flanders)

The tapestry depicts shepherds and shepherdess playing children’s game ‘la main chaude’, known as ‘hot cockles’ in English. One participant hides their face in somebody’s lap with one hand behind his back. The other players slap the hand making it ‘hot’ while the hand’s owner has to guess who is slapping.

The Three Fates

(early 16th century, Netherlands)

The fragment of a larger tapestry depict poem I Trionfi by Italian poet Petrarch. It describes his allegorical vision. In mythology Fates controlled the span of human life; Clotho is a spinner, Lachesis was the drawer of the lots, and Atropos represented the inevitable end to life.

The Triumph of Eternity over Time

(c. 1510-1520, Tournai)

The fragment of a larger tapestry is based on a poem I Trionfi by Italian poet Petrarch. The panel includes the back wheel of the chariot of Eternity, and crushed beneath it are two female figures, Lachesis and Clotho, two of the Fates

Rustic sports tapestry

(1500-1515, Flanders)

This tapestry depicts different forms of hunting: with greyhounds, with a falcon and with a crossbow. It also includes a sly pun on the idea of the hunt, showing men pursuing women with sexual intent.

Falconry

(1430’s, Arras/Low Countries)

The foreground is filled with tiny trees, bushes and flowers, rocky caves for bears and streams with water-fowl. In this setting the huntsmen and their prey crowd across the tapestry in two great tiers subtly linked by intermediate figures, which lead the eye from group to group. 'Falconry' is the tapestry which focuses on a single hunt.

The Devonshire Hunting Tapestries

(1430’s, Arras/Low Countries)

The otter hunt shown in this tapestry is rather gruesome. Otters were hunted not to eat but for their skins and because they ate the fish that were needed for the table. Swan meat was, however, highly desirable and here boys are seen robbing a swan’s nest. At one time, swans could be owned only by the royal family but as they gradually escaped into the wild they became fair game for other people, although theoretically they could only be hunted by licence. A ferocious bear hunt is seen on the right with oriental figures wearing turbans.

The Devonshire Hunting Tapestries

(1440-1450)

By the 15th century all types of hunting had their own rules. Deer hunting was known as the sport of kings and confined to the courts and other favoured individuals. In this tapestry the main scenes show various parts of the deer hunt, including, at the centre bottom, a dead deer being cut up and the meat distributed. This was carried out as a set ritual with specific cuts of the meat being given to particular huntsmen and favoured dogs.

Esther and Ahasuerus

(c. 1510-1520, Brussels)

This tapestry depicts a scene from the Old Testament story of Esther and Ahasuerus. The subject was a favourite theme in tapestries, particularly in the15th and 16th century.
